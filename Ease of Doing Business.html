AN OVERVIEW OF SABER AND THE EASE OF DOING BUSINESS IN NIGERIA

NOVEMBER | 2022
The Ease of Doing Business was published by the world bank. It is an aggregate figure that includes distinct parameters used in measuring the factors that affect business activities that describe the Ease of doing business.
The Ease of Doing Business involves ten (10) indicators that affect business regulations, including: starting a business, Getting credit, Dealing with construction permits, Protecting minority investors, Enforcing contracts, Paying taxes, Getting electricity, Resolving insolvency, Registering property, and trading across borders.
Based on the most recent world bank annual ratings, Nigeria ranks 131 along with 190 economies in the Ease of doing business. The rank in Nigeria improved significantly to 131 in 2019 from 146 in 2018.

What is the SABER Program?

State Actions on Business Environment Reforms (SABER) is a 3-year operation-based intervention, jointly designed by the world bank and the PEBEC secretariat with assistance from the federal ministry of finance, budget and planning, and the Nigeria Governors’ forum (NGF) secretariat.
The objective is to strengthen the implementation of business-enabling reforms in Nigeria, specifically across the participating states of the federation and the FCT.

How can the SABER program improve the Ease of doing business in Nigeria?

The SABER program is responsible for improving the Ease of doing business in Nigeria, and the improvements are already visible.
Above is a chart that outlines Nigeria’s Ease of doing business in 2020. The improvements recorded in 2020 include Starting a business, Getting credit, dealing with construction permits, protecting minority investors, enforcing contracts, and paying taxes. To develop the Ease of doing business in Nigeria, SABER must do the following:
  • Electricity: Nigeria has improved in this aspect over the years. However, extra work still needs to be done. Nigeria is yet to attain a 24-hour uninterrupted power supply, which is a significant factor affecting the Ease of business in Nigeria.
  • Resolving insolvency: SABER has to work on resolving the issue of bankruptcy among businesses in Nigeria to develop the Ease of doing business in Nigeria
  • Registering Property: Nigeria has enhanced its land administration through a geographic information system. However, the work is far from being completed.
  • Trading across borders: An electronic payment of fees has been introduced, which has significantly reduced the time for complying with documentary and border regulations in Nigeria to 564 hours in 2020 compared to 662.1 hours in 2019. More work still needs to be done. We are still lagging behind in this field despite our previous improvement.
In conclusion, Nigeria is a developing country with a population of 2.64% of the world population. It will be strenuous to improve the Ease of doing business in Nigeria. Nevertheless, it is not impossible as we have so many young, motivated, and hard workers in the country. With our endowment in Manpower, Nigeria can increase its position. Still, there must be an ongoing and apparent changes to ensure these improvements influences the Ease of doing business in Nigeria.
